Keyboard playing

I have a pretty long story with "Maestro Gombau" public music school in Getafe. I studied music and keyboard playing for 9 years, playing in bands for 6 of those (at least 4 in Salida de Emergencia). I took a 4 year break to get more into electronic music making, and I came back as a keyboardist to the music school in September 2019.

Right now I'm playing the keyboards (mainly pianos and EPs, but also some organs and synth pads here and there) in a pop/country/funk band.

Electronic music

I've been into electronic music not that long. I was mainly into different kinds of Metal thanks to my father, and around 2013 I first listened to a Brostep track. The uniqueness and totally different focus of the sound really intrigued me, and it also shared the same raw power of the music I liked. From there I discovered the commercial side of Drum & Bass (mind you, apart from the beat pattern and tempo, they are pretty much the same), and years after I fell into the more underground scenes. I didn't get the thing with House music until 2015 or so with the rise of Future House (and I discovered it thanks to my brother).

I make electronic music and I've been doing it since 2014. You can find more details about that below. I work with Reaper and Renoise, and an assortment of plugins. Reaper allows me to quickly work with and edit audio recordings, as well as MIDI clips. It's my "main" DAW, and the best things about it are the performance and flexibility of the program itself - the programming standards of the developer seem to align really well with what I think it's the "ideal", even the licensing system (affordable price and no online requirements, only a license file). Renoise is also a really good piece of software (best in mangling samples and creating rhythms), and both are rock solid and light on resources (and work on Linux!), as well as full of functionality and technical detail.

As for plugins, I love Sugar Bytes synths. They have an amazing user experience and interface, and are a perfect mix of regular synthesizer components (filters and oscillators) and innovative features (weird modulation sources and sequencing). They are not too weird, so you can actually create interesting and usable sounds from them. ArcSyn also gets a lot of use, when I need to go back to a "regular" synth architecture. In terms of effects, I have a mixed folder of commercial, paid (nice) plugins (Klanghelm and HoRNet) and free plugins. I prefer to use the native ones when possible, though (especially on Renoise where projects are self contained ZIPs and I can easily move them between machines without worrying on plugin and sample availability).

My workspace

This is the equipment I have at home, and this is where I make most of my stuff. I use a PC with Reaper as my main DAW, with Renoise as the secondary (actually I'm making most of my electronic music with Renoise lately). As for plugins I have some Sugar Bytes synths, ArcSyn and a few cheap and free effects (Klanghelm, Tokyo Dawn Labs, several giveaways and promotions...). I also have a big load of samples to use and mangle.

All my music is made "in the box" and I try to keep the usage of plugins to the minimum, at least in Renoise. Also almost all the audio software I use is online DRM free (with serial number or license file based authorization systems) and as portable as possible (they are either a .dll or .vst3 you can just drop on a fresh install, or the installer does not need internet connection). I feel this is very important when making music on Windows - if you can avoid connecting your computer to the internet you will avoid several background processes messing with the performance and the OS.
