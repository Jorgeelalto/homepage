The most valuable traits for music hardware, in my opinion, are:

  • User experience defines how I interact with the device. This is the main point of using hardware, right? Even if it doesn't sound better than software, you find yourself using it for the results it guides you towards.
  • Sound, not necessarily sound quality per se, but the style it tends to create.
  • Build quality, makes you feel like you are interacting with an actual instrument or a toy that will break as soon as you do something wrong. A better build inspires.

I will use a 3 point scoring system. 3 means I would keep or get it again. 2 means it was OK for a while but I wouldn't repeat. 1 means it was just bad

Soma Lyra-8

 UX: A real instrument. Not that hard to understand what is happening, but a lot of uncertainty makes it evolve easily.
3/3 Sound: Raw and noisy, what you imagine an analog synthesizer to be. Horror drones and melodic atmospheres. You have to interact with it to give it movement. Limited range.
Build: A tank. Best synth you will ever lay your hands on in terms of heft. Knobs are tight. Made to last and be repaired forever.

Minilogue XD

 UX: Classic substractive synth, weighed down by the preset system (relative knobs). 99% of the functions you need are in the front panel. Controls are well calibrated for wide sweet spots. Can't make an ugly noise.
3/3 Sound: Pristine, ready for recording. Stereo is wide enough, effects are pretty, everything is usable. Analog oscillators are ideal. An actual tool.
Build: It wants to be good. Front panel is metal and controls are tight, but the part that you don't see, namely the back part, is cheap plastic. Relatively lightweight, which doesn't inspire confidence.

Digitone mk1

 UX: Looks like it is not an inspiration machine, but with the sequencer, scales and arpeggiator it reaches a great mid point between programming and playing.
3/3 Sound: Beautiful. Not hard to program nice sounds, and has a very defined palette. The sequencer is part of the generation because of parameter locks. Well thought out, no digital artifacts, flexible.
Build: Really good but the sticky buttons are a big problem that hold it down from feeling like a pro machine. Easy to open and clean.

Pico System III

 UX: Well thought out. Follows the usual signal's path, there is always room for more cables. Toyish though, you wonder how to use it in real music apart from FX recordings.
2/3 Sound: A bit dirty but versatile. The analogue artifacts and limits of each module make it a very interesting "no barriers" sound generator. Hard to overcome the limits of the delay, greatly benefits from external reverb/delay.
Build: Good for the size. The case is solid, minijack plugs are screwed on, but knobs are a bit loose. Not a problem but not a tank either.

M-Audio Venom

 UX: Preset tweaker. Everything you can touch is cheap plastic. Knobs are very loose.
1/3 Sound: Versatile, but muffled in a weird way. Has the potential but something is blocking the way.
Build: Very cheap. Not good.

Erebus V3 OG

 UX: The lack of input devices kills it for me. The fact that you have to plan ahead to enjoy this synth makes it a pain to use.
2/3 Sound: Good. I honestly don't remember it too much. It was a bit hard to find sweet spots.
Build: Great. Felt handmade which is cool.
